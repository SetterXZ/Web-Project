Overview

What is the James Webb Space Telescope?

JWST is the largest and most powerful space telescope ever built, designed to see the universe in infrared light. Launched on December 25, 2021, it now orbits 1.5 million km from Earth at the second Lagrange point (L2).

JWST's primary mirror is made of 18 hexagonal beryllium segments, each coated in a thin layer of gold to maximize infrared reflectivity. Together they form a 6.5-meter aperture — too large to fit inside any rocket fairing in one piece.

The telescope was folded like origami for launch and slowly unfolded over two weeks in space. But unfolding is only the beginning — each of the 18 mirrors had to be aligned to within a fraction of the wavelength of light.

Key Goal
The 18 segments must work together as a single mirror with a wavefront error of less than 150 nanometers RMS — thinner than a human hair divided into 500 slices.

The Challenge

Why Is Alignment So Hard?

Imagine trying to assemble a jigsaw puzzle 1.5 million km away, in zero gravity, at –233°C, where each piece must sit at exactly the right height and angle — accurate to a few nanometers.

The Three Degrees of Error

Each mirror segment can be misaligned in three key ways that degrade image quality:

① Tip & Tilt
The segment is tilted at the wrong angle — its light goes to the wrong spot on the detector. Corrected by 2 actuators per segment.
② Piston (Dephasing)
The segment sits at the wrong height along the optical axis — light from adjacent segments travels different path lengths, causing destructive interference. Corrected by 1 actuator.
③ Clocking, Radius of Curvature & Astigmatism
Higher-order shape errors of the mirror surface. Corrected by 3 additional actuators (6 total per segment, plus a radius-of-curvature actuator = 7).
The very first JWST image: 18 separate, unfocused spots of the star HD 84406. Each dot = one mirror segment. Credit: NASA/STScI
Scale of Precision
Each segment must be positioned to within ±10 nm vertically. At 6.5 m across, this is equivalent to measuring across a continent while allowing a tolerance of just 1 mm.
The Physics

The Equations Behind Perfect Vision

Optical alignment is governed by wave optics. Here are the four core equations that drive every decision in the JWST alignment campaign.

Rayleigh Criterion — Angular Resolution
\[ \theta_{\min} = 1.22\,\frac{\lambda}{D} \]
  • θ_min — minimum angular separation resolvable (radians)
  • λ — wavelength of light
  • D — diameter of the mirror
JWST example: At λ = 2 µm, D = 6.5 m:
θ_min ≈ 0.064 arcseconds — 7× sharper than Hubble at the same wavelength. This limit is only achievable if all 18 segments act as one coherent mirror.
Strehl Ratio — Image Quality Metric
\[ S = \exp\!\left[-\!\left(\frac{2\pi\,\sigma_{\rm rms}}{\lambda}\right)^{\!2}\right] \]
  • S — Strehl ratio (1.0 = perfect, >0.8 = diffraction-limited)
  • σ_rms — RMS wavefront error
  • λ — wavelength of light
JWST target: σ_rms < 150 nm at λ = 2 µm gives S > 0.85 — meaning the telescope delivers >85% of the theoretical peak brightness.
Optical Path Difference & Phase
\[ \Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\cdot\mathrm{OPD} \]
  • Δφ — phase difference between two rays (radians)
  • OPD — optical path length difference
  • λ — wavelength of light
A piston error δ creates OPD = 2δ (factor of 2: light reflects). Constructive interference requires OPD = nλ. For δ = 1 µm and λ = 2 µm, Δφ = π → complete destructive interference. This is why piston control is critical.
RMS Wavefront Error
\[ \sigma_{\rm rms} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(W_i - \bar{W}\right)^2} \]
  • W_i — wavefront error at point i on the mirror
  • W̄ — mean wavefront error
  • N — number of sample points
JWST measures the wavefront using phase retrieval: defocused images of a star are mathematically inverted to reconstruct the 3D wavefront shape across all 18 segments simultaneously.
The Maréchale Criterion
A system is considered diffraction-limited when its RMS wavefront error satisfies σ_rms < λ/14. At JWST's primary wavelength of 2.1 µm, this means σ_rms < 150 nm. JWST achieved σ_rms ≈ 57 nm — beating the requirement by nearly 3×.
Step-by-Step

The 7 Stages of JWST Mirror Alignment

Alignment is a hierarchical process: coarse errors are fixed first, then progressively finer corrections are applied — like tuning an orchestra section by section before conducting the full symphony.

1
Segment Image Stacking
Feb 2022 NIRCam Coarse
The telescope points at a bright, isolated star (HD 84406, magnitude ~6.4). NIRCam's detector is scanned across a 2° × 2° field of view to locate all 18 separate star images — one from each mirror segment. At this stage, the images are scattered and severely out of focus.
Physics at work
Each segment acts as a small independent telescope. The 18 images are spread over the detector because the segments have large tip/tilt errors. The angular position of each dot maps directly to the tilt error of its corresponding segment: Δθ = f(tip, tilt actuator displacement).
Stage 1: 18 separated images of the star HD 84406, one per mirror segment. Credit: NASA/STScI
2
Segment Alignment (Image Array → Mosaic)
Tip/Tilt Correction 7 actuators/segment
Engineers command the actuators behind each segment to tilt it until all 18 star images move into the center of the detector field. The result is an array of 18 images close together — not yet overlapping. This corrects the tip and tilt degrees of freedom.
Physics at work
Tilting a mirror segment by angle α displaces its image by: \[ \Delta x = 2\alpha \cdot f \] where \(f\) is the telescope focal length. JWST's focal length is ~131 m, so a 1 µrad tilt moves the image by ~0.26 mm on the detector.
3
Image Stacking
Overlap NIRCam
Fine tip/tilt corrections bring all 18 images onto the same spot on the detector. The image becomes much brighter (18× more flux) but still blurry — the 18 mirrors now point at the same place but are not yet coherent (their wavefronts don't match up in phase).
Physics at work
When 18 incoherent sources overlap, intensity adds: \[ I_{\rm total} = \sum_{i=1}^{18} I_i = 18\,I_0 \] But for a fully aligned, coherent mirror, amplitude adds: \[ I_{\rm coherent} = \left|\sum_{i=1}^{18} A_i\right|^2 = 18^2\,I_0 \] Achieving coherence (phasing) unlocks 18× more peak intensity.
Stage 3: All 18 images stacked at the detector center. Still blurry — phase alignment not yet done. Credit: NASA/STScI
4
Coarse Phasing
Piston Correction Dispersed Fringe Sensor
The most critical step: correcting piston errors — the height differences between adjacent segments along the optical axis. The Dispersed Fringe Sensor (DFS) within NIRCam uses a prism to spread light into a spectrum. If two adjacent segments have a piston error, the spectrum shows a characteristic fringe pattern whose spacing encodes the OPD.
Physics at work — Dispersed Fringe Sensing
A piston error δ between two adjacent segments produces a path difference OPD = 2δ. In the dispersed spectrum, this creates an intensity oscillation: \[ I(\lambda) = I_0\left[1 + \cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi\cdot 2\delta}{\lambda}\right)\right] \] By measuring the fringe period as a function of wavelength, engineers solve directly for δ. After correction: piston errors < a few wavelengths (~5 µm → <1 µm).
Why Coarse First?
Fine phasing (wavefront sensing) only works when piston errors are already smaller than ~few µm. Coarse phasing first reduces gross errors by 1000×, bringing the system into the capture range of fine phasing.
5
Fine Phasing — Wavefront Sensing & Control
Phase Retrieval < 150 nm RMS target Iterative
A series of deliberately defocused images of a star are taken at different focal planes. The known geometry of the optics allows the software to run a phase retrieval algorithm (similar to Gerchberg–Saxton) to reconstruct the full wavefront error map across all 18 segments. Actuator corrections are then calculated and applied. This loop is repeated until σ_rms < 150 nm.
Physics at work — Phase Retrieval
The Fourier relationship between pupil and focal planes: \[ U_{\rm focal}(x,y) = \mathcal{F}\!\left[U_{\rm pupil}(\xi,\eta)\right] \] where \(U_{\rm pupil} = A(\xi,\eta)\,e^{i W(\xi,\eta)}\). The detector measures intensity \(|U_{\rm focal}|^2\), but not the phase. Phase retrieval recovers \(W(\xi,\eta)\) from multiple defocused intensity images using iterative Fourier transforms between the pupil and image planes.
Stage 5: The star after fine phasing — now approaching diffraction-limited sharpness. Credit: NASA/STScI
6
Telescope Alignment to Science Instruments
Secondary Mirror NIRSpec, MIRI, NIRISS, FGS
The secondary mirror (SM) is a 74 cm convex mirror on a tripod structure. Its position along 6 degrees of freedom is adjusted to bring the telescope into focus for all four science instruments simultaneously. Each instrument's field of view is then individually aligned using the Fine Steering Mirror (FSM).
Physics at work
Moving the secondary mirror by Δz along the optical axis shifts focus by: \[ \Delta f = \frac{M^2\,\Delta z}{1} \] where \(M \approx 18\) is the secondary mirror magnification. This 18× lever arm means even a 1 mm SM move shifts focus by ~18 mm — very sensitive and requiring precise control.
7
Iterate — Multifield & Multwavelength Fine Phasing
Final Polish 57 nm RMS achieved
Fine phasing is repeated multiple times at different field positions and wavelengths to correct global residual aberrations and ensure uniform image quality across the full field of view. JWST also uses the Fine Guidance Sensor (FGS) for wavefront sensing to maintain alignment during science operations.
Final Result
JWST's total wavefront error budget of 150 nm RMS breaks down as: \[ \sigma_{\rm total}^2 = \sigma_{\rm mirror}^2 + \sigma_{\rm alignment}^2 + \sigma_{\rm stability}^2 + \cdots \] Each term must be tightly controlled. The final achieved value: σ_rms ≈ 57 nm — nearly 3× better than the requirement. The Strehl ratio at 2 µm ≈ 0.97.
